Digest (<=8 bullets)

- Adjuvant platinum-based chemotherapy improves survival in early-stage epithelial ovarian cancer overall, but randomized trials did not prospectively stratify by histologic subtype; high‑grade endometrioid carcinoma behaves similarly to high‑grade serous and worse than low‑grade endometrioid, so is often managed with adjuvant chemotherapy analogous to high‑grade serous disease [ESGO/ESMO/ESP 2024, 1].  

- When planning therapy for recurrent disease, “platinum‑sensitive” vs “platinum‑resistant” should be treated as a continuum; clinical judgment and flexibility are required when assigning category and choosing treatment, rather than strict time‑cutoffs alone [NCCN Ovary v3.2025, 2].  

- At relapse, selection between PARP inhibitor and anti‑VEGF–based strategies should consider histologic subtype, BRCA1/2 mutation status, prior exposure to PARP inhibitors and/or bevacizumab, and cumulative prior platinum exposure, as these factors influence benefit and toxicity [ESMO Epithelial Ovarian 2023, 3].  

- For first recurrence after initial platinum‑based therapy, platinum‑based combination chemotherapy is preferred (category 1 evidence), with consideration of secondary cytoreductive surgery in appropriate candidates and integration of best supportive care where indicated [NCCN Ovary v3.2025, 4].  

- In selected cases of recurrent disease, immediate platinum‑based therapy at first recurrence is supported (category 2B) as an alternative strategy, again with individualized use of best supportive care and/or surgery based on overall goals and fitness [NCCN Ovary v3.2025, 4].  

- For sex cord‑stromal tumours, routine retroperitoneal lymph node dissection is not required because retroperitoneal metastases are rare; management should instead focus on pelvic/abdominal disease and mandatory uterine evaluation (endometrial sampling or hysterectomy) to exclude concomitant uterine cancer in granulosa cell tumours [ESMO Non‑epithelial Ovarian 2018, 5].
