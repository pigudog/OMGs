Digest (<=8 bullets)

    
- NCCN defines recurrent ovarian cancer platinum sensitivity on a spectrum based on platinum-free interval; treatment selection should use clinical judgment rather than rigid cutoffs, recognizing overlap between “sensitive” and “resistant” categories [@guideline:nccn_ovariancancer_v3_2025__v00000000|14].  

- For platinum-resistant disease (including cases where further platinum is not appropriate), standard of care is single-agent non-platinum chemotherapy (e.g., weekly paclitaxel, PLD, topotecan, gemcitabine) with or without bevacizumab; responses are modest and prognosis poor, underscoring need to consider clinical trials when available [@guideline:nccn_ovariancancer_v3_2025__v00000000|14; @pubmed:37916177].  

- Bevacizumab-containing regimens are guideline-supported in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer for patients without contraindications, improving progression-free survival when added to single-agent chemotherapy compared with chemotherapy alone [@guideline:nccn_ovariancancer_v3_2025__v00000000|14].  

- Re-treatment with platinum-based chemotherapy is generally reserved for platinum-sensitive relapse; NCCN notes limited benefit and increased toxicity from further platinum in clearly platinum-resistant or platinum-refractory disease and recommends non-platinum regimens in that setting [@guideline:nccn_ovariancancer_v3_2025__v00000000|14–15].  

- Targeted therapy and immunotherapy may be considered in select, heavily pretreated or rare-histology ovarian cancers when actionable molecular alterations or high TMB/MSI-H are present; case-level evidence suggests potential benefit in platinum-refractory settings but data remain sparse and such use is typically in clinical trial or off-label context [@pubmed:29983579].  

- Experimental approaches to predicting platinum resistance (e.g., tissue platinum distribution, deep learning histopathologic classifiers) show promise for stratifying patients before or early in treatment but are not yet standard of care; MDTs should consider these only in research or specialized centers [@pubmed:35296733; @pubmed:38762636].  

- Malignant ovarian germ cell tumors are managed differently from epithelial ovarian cancer: fertility-sparing surgery plus platinum-based chemotherapy yields high cure rates across stages, and treatment should aim to preserve fertility when oncologically safe [@pubmed:33490225].
